The S.O.I.L. Method — a four-step approach to Scripture

The Bible is a collection of 66 books, written at different times, in different places, to different audiences. In order to study it well, a student must first seek to understand what the original author wrote to the original audience — only then can we explore how it applies today. The S.O.I.L. Method gives us a simple, repeatable framework for doing exactly that.

One simple mistake to avoid: moving too fast. The Bible is a rich and deep book — spend most of your time observing before you try to interpret.

S · Select O · Observe I · Interpret L · Apply
S

Step One

Select the Text

The goal of this step is to learn as much as possible about the background of the selected text. This means stepping back and asking questions about the text as a whole — before you read a single word of content.

Questions to Ask

  • Who was the original author of this text?
  • Who was the original audience?
  • Where does this text fit into the whole Bible's story?
  • What comes before and after the text?
  • When did the events of this text take place?
  • What is the literary genre of the text? (History? Poetry? Letter? Prophecy?)
A good study Bible or Bible dictionary can help you answer these background questions quickly. Knowing the context changes everything.

Discussion Questions

  1. 1Why does it matter who the original audience was before we apply a passage to ourselves?
  2. 2How might knowing the literary genre of a passage change how we read it?
  3. 3What resources do you currently use to understand the background of a Bible passage?
O

Step Two — 50% of Your Time

Observe the Text

The goal of this step is to interrogate the text and look for the "who," "where," "when," and "what" details. This step is all about seeing what is there — do not try to make sense of it yet. Just look carefully and write down everything you find.

What to Look For

Who?

  • A single name: David, Abraham, Peter
  • A people group: Jews, Gentiles, Egyptians
  • An implied group: everyone, angels, soldiers
  • A person's title: pharaoh, pharisee, servant

Where?

  • City or country: Jerusalem, Judea, Canaan, Rome
  • Specific location: upper room, Jordan river, Temple

When?

  • A specific time: 9 in the morning, the 6th hour
  • Festivals: Pentecost, Sabbath, Passover
  • Implied time: next day, evening, immediately
  • Event time: when he heard, after saying this

What?

  • Objects: sheep, bread, stones, water
  • Actions: sat down, taught, baptized, fought
  • Themes: prayer, forgiveness, sin, grace
Spend half of your total study time here. The more carefully you observe, the more accurately you will interpret. Most Bible study errors come from skipping this step.

Discussion Questions

  1. 4Why is it important to separate observation from interpretation? What happens when we mix them too quickly?
  2. 5Pick a short passage — John 3:1-5. Practice: what "who," "where," "when," and "what" details can you find?
  3. 6How does slowing down at this stage help protect us from wrong teaching?
I

Step Three — 30% of Your Time

Interpret the Text

The goal of this step is to make sense of the observations from step two. Now that you have a list of what you have "seen," you need to begin to understand what those observations mean. Ask literary, historical, and theological questions.

Literary Questions

  • Are there key words, terms, or repeated themes?
  • Does the original language (Hebrew or Greek) reveal anything?
  • Are there words used elsewhere in the Bible that connect to this passage?

Historical Questions

  • When and where did this passage take place?
  • What is the political or cultural landscape at the time?
  • Where is this passage in the big Bible story?

Theological Questions

  • What does this passage teach about God?
  • What does this passage teach about salvation?
  • What does this passage teach about humanity?
  • What does this passage teach about the Church?
The goal of interpretation is always to find the one meaning the original author intended. A passage cannot mean something to us today that it could not have meant to the original audience.

Discussion Questions

  1. 7What is the difference between what a passage means and what it means to me? Why does that distinction matter?
  2. 8How can comparing a passage with other Scriptures help us interpret it correctly?
  3. 9Can you think of a passage that is commonly misinterpreted? What goes wrong in the interpretation process?
L

Step Four — Life Application

Apply the Text to Life

The goal of this final step is to ask how the text could apply to one's personal life. This is the only step that has the modern perspective in view. Only after we know what the passage meant to its original audience can we ask what it means for us today.

Questions to Ask

  • How can this passage apply to my life today?
  • What principles in the text carry forward into modern life?
  • Does the passage teach anything for the Church today?
  • Does the passage speak to any social issues in my community?
  • Can I share encouragement or challenge from this passage with others?
Application is the goal of all Bible study — but it must come last. Getting to application too quickly is the most common mistake. Do the work first, then ask: what does God want me to do with this?

Discussion Questions

  1. 10Why must application always come after careful observation and interpretation?
  2. 11How can the S.O.I.L. Method help you prepare better sermons and teaching for your congregation?
  3. 12How will you begin to use this method in your personal Bible study this week?

The S.O.I.L. Method at a Glance

A simple, repeatable method for studying any passage in the Bible. Follow the steps in order — do not rush to application.

S

Select — 10%

Choose your passage and gather background information about the author, audience, and context.

O

Observe — 50%

Look carefully at who, where, when, and what. Do not interpret yet — just see what is there.

I

Interpret — 30%

Ask literary, historical, and theological questions to discover what the passage meant to its original audience.

L

Life Application — 10%

Only now ask: what does God want me to do with this truth today?
